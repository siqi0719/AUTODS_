# Executive Summary

This report translates the AutoDS Pipeline model evaluation results into actionable business guidance for our real estate analytics product. The XGBoost regression model has been validated as the optimal solution for predicting weekly rent (AUD) across Australia’s six major capital cities, achieving a test RMSE of $17.31 — meaning predictions are, on average, within ±$17.31 of actual rent. With an R² of 0.785, the model explains nearly 79% of the variability in rental pricing using available listing data. Crucially, it runs in under 2.1 seconds per prediction and maintains strong generalisation — making it production-ready for integration into client-facing tools (e.g., rent estimation dashboards, landlord pricing assistants, or agent listing optimisation modules). This capability directly supports our core business goal: delivering accurate, city-aware, and operationally scalable rental intelligence to property professionals across Australia.

## 1. Key Findings

- **Suburb is the dominant driver of rent**: “suburb_sydney” alone accounts for 26.0% of total feature importance — more than all other features combined except point_of_contact and floor_area. Sydney listings command a substantial premium relative to other cities, and this effect is captured robustly by the model.
- **Point of contact matters commercially**: “point_of_contact_contact_agent” (7.8%) and “point_of_contact_contact_owner” (5.7%) rank as the 2nd and 3rd most important features — indicating that *who* lists the property (agent vs. owner) systematically influences market pricing, likely reflecting differences in presentation, targeting, or negotiation power.
- **Physical attributes remain foundational but secondary**: Floor area (5.2%), number of bedrooms (2.6%), and number of bathrooms (1.8%) are confirmed as material drivers — yet their combined importance (9.6%) is less than suburb alone. This underscores that location and listing context outweigh raw property specs in pricing power.
- **Low-value noise features persist**: 24 features (including “street_suffix_RARE”, “advertised_date_year”, “point_of_contact_RARE”) have zero or near-zero importance (<0.001). These were retained due to encoding decisions but contribute no predictive signal — increasing model complexity without benefit.
- **Data quality is high but limited in scale**: After cleaning, 2,813 listings remain (81.9% retention), with zero missing values and no duplicates — however, the dataset covers only ~3,400 listings across six cities, limiting granularity for suburb-level submarkets (e.g., inner-Sydney vs. western-Sydney) and rare property types.

## 4. Risk Notes

- **Geographic bias risk**: With “suburb_sydney” contributing 26% of importance and Sydney being the highest-rent city, the model may over-index on Sydney patterns. If new listings from regional NSW or Darwin are added without retraining, predictions could drift — requiring quarterly geographic performance audits.
- **Privacy & compliance exposure**: Although PII columns (phone_number, email, first_name, last_name) were dropped during cleaning, “prefix” (e.g., “Dr”, “Ms”, “Mr”) and “gender” retain weak demographic signals. Their inclusion (even at low importance: 1.4% combined) poses unnecessary privacy risk under Australian Privacy Principles (APP 11) and should be removed entirely before production deployment.
- **Operational brittleness**: The model relies on “level” encoding (e.g., “level_ground_out_of_1”, “level_2_out_of_3”), which assumes consistent parsing of level descriptions across listing sources. If feed providers change formatting (e.g., “Ground Floor” → “G” or “Lobby”), encoded levels will misalign — triggering silent degradation. A validation step must verify level string consistency pre-ingestion.
- **Commercial misinterpretation risk**: High importance of “point_of_contact” reflects correlation, not causation — i.e., agents list higher-value properties, not that hiring an agent raises rent. Without clear documentation, clients may incorrectly infer that switching to an agent will increase rent — exposing us to reputational and contractual risk.
